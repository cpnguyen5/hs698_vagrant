Cancer Risk Factors

This section explores potential cancer risk factors present in the CMS dataset. Primarily, the demographics will be examined to identify any trends amongst certain cohorts for the prevalence of cancer.

Return to Cancer Overview

Number of Patients Diagnosed with Cancer

Age

The distribution of cancer prevalence is explored across each age group of the CMS dataset.

  • The median for the number of beneficiaries with cancer is approximately uniform across all age groups.
  • Age groups 65-74 and 75-84 demonstrate higher variation as well as a larger interquartile range. This may indicate that these groups have a higher distribution for higher prevalence of cancer.Additionally, these groups have higher whisker values/bounds.
  • Age groups <65 and >84 have a smaller interquartile range, suggesting that the prevalence values are in more agreement or consistent within these groups.

Race

The distribution of cancer prevalence is explored across each race/ethnicity group.

  • Non-hispanic White beneficiaries have an exceptionally larger interquartile range and upper whisker bound, indicating a higher variation of cancer prevalence amongst CMS beneficiaries of this racial background.
  • The other racial groups have lower distributions and hence, prevalence of cancer. The lower interquartile range (shorter box) suggests the prevalence of cancer is more consistent amongst these groups.
  • Note: The dataset is limited due its lack of diversity with its surveyed population consisting primarily of white beneficiaries.

Cancer Mortality Rate by Race

The supplementary Big Cities Health Coalition dataset is used to examined the mortality rate of cancer in each major metropolitan. The mortality rates will provide a more in-depth understanding of the effects of cancer in the community.

Note: The mortality rates are reported per 100,000 people. The dataset is limited to only surveying large cities and the following cancer types and respective ICD10 codes:

  • C00-C07: all cancers
  • C33-C34: lung cancer
  • C50: breast cancer

Annual Average Mortality Rate

The trajectory of cancer mortality rates for each race.

  • The Big Cities Health Coalition dataset shows that African Americans have higher mortality rates as a result of cancer in the big cities. White Americans are consistently second in leading for mortality rates.
  • Asians & Pacific Islanders and Hispanics are surveyed to have significantly lower mortality rates as a result of cancer in the big cities.
  • The spikes in mortality rates as a result of cancer for Native Americans and Other races may be due to their lack of representation in the previous surveyed years.

Overall Mortality Rate

The overall average mortality rate compared across the race/ethnicity groups.

  • African Americans lead all other racial groups for highest mortality rate from cancer in the big cities. Followed by people of White and Native American backgrounds.
  • Asian & Pacific Islanders and Hispanics generally have the lowest mortality rate.
